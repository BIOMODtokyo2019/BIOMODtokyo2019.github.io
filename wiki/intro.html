Background

Recently, swarm robotics has been gathering attention because swarm robots are more flexible and autonomous than single robots in many cases. The movement of swarm robots was inspired by the group behavior of ants and fish and so on. For example, a research group in Harvard University is trying to develop the ‘thousand-robot swarm'. In this system, more than 1000 robots form a programmed figure autonomously.
Swarm robots can be useful in many fields, and one of the fields is medicine. By administering the robots in our body, many kinds of data can be taken, and swarm robots can assist fertilization and delivering drugs. However, it is difficult to realise nano-scale electrical robots which is needed for application into medicine. Additionally, the robots should give complicated responses for functioning in vivo. Therefore, we suggest nano-scale swarm robots made of biomolecules. Biomolecules such as DNAs are small enough to make nano-scale robots, and DNA strands enable the robots to move complicatedly and logically because of the complementary binding.
In previous research, the convenience of molecular sensing, using the aggregation of gold nano particle whose surface was modified with DNA strands, was indicated. By using this technology, higher sensitivity is realized. In our project, swarm robots are made of beads and DNA strands, and more practical swarm movement was set as the objective of the research.

Objective

The object of our project is to make nano-scale swarm robot which amplify the target DNAs under the isothermal condition and detect them by the logic gate.
In conventional DNA amplification, high temperature cycle is necessary. However, high temperature cycle is inconvenient in vivo or in vitro in some cases. For example, isothermal amplification and detection of the sequence enables us to detect low concentration DNAs in vivo without killing cells or protein denaturation. Also, it gives us new method of amplification of the specific DNA sequence using the heat-sensitive materials.
Additionally, the novelty of our swarm robot is the way of increasing and visualizing the signal. There are two steps of increase. The first step is the amplification of the DNA. Utilizing the elongation by polymerase and the cutting by nicking enzyme, DNA strands as signal are amplified. The second step is aggregation. DNA signal is converted into aggregation of beads, and the volume of fluorescence increase. Through these processes, we can detect low concentrate DNA.
As described above, our objective nano-scale swarm robot is worth developing because of its isothermal amplification and high sensitivity.
